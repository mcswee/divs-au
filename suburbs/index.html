How to pronounce the names of Melbourne suburbs

Most pronunciation guides for Melbourne are useless because they rely on lazy approximations and only cover the "greatest hits." They’ll list the same ten or twenty suburbs like Prahran, Malvern, or Reservoir, and then hedge their bets on Lalor by giving you two options. They focus on the popular traps while completely missing the rest of the city—places like Kealba, Clematis, or Eumemmering don't even get a look in.

These guides also mangle the actual sounds of the city. Representing Melbourne as "Mel-bin" is a phonological lie; it implies the same vowel as a rubbish bin, which is just wrong. It’s clearly a schwa.

This list is a complete, systematic record of every Melbourne suburb using a consistent phonological framework. It maps the city exactly as it's spoken by locals, using a one-to-one system to capture the Melburnian accent without the guesswork of figuring out the cot/caught merger.

A key to the pronunciation is provided at the bottom of this page, to help explain what all these symbols mean.

Pronunciation key and orthography

This guide uses a custom phonetic system designed to represent the specific nuances of the Melbourne accent. Syllables are separated by dots (·), and specific characters are used to represent sounds that standard English spelling often obscures.

These pronunciations are based on my accent in Melburnian Australian English. To ensure clarity, the system follows a strict logic:

  • Vowels: Every vowel sound uses either a unique symbol (like @ for the schwa, or æ for a flat 'a') or a dedicated two-character combination. It is important to note that these two-character pairings are not necessarily diphthongs; they are single units used to ensure every vowel sound is distinct and consistent.
  • Consonants: All consonants use a single symbol or letter. For example, the sound £ is used specifically for the 'ng' sound (ŋ).
